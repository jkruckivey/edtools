Can You Calculate Sponsorship ROI?

Nike pays $500M+ per year in sponsorships. Can they prove it works? This week, you'll learn how brands justify billion-dollar partnerships when perfect attribution is impossible.

Before diving into sponsorship and betting frameworks, test your current knowledge. This diagnostic reveals where to focus your learning energy this week.

Challenge 1: Understand Sponsorship Economics

The Problem: Your CFO asks: "We spent $50M on sponsorships last year. What did we get for it?" Can you explain how brands measure sponsorship value?

What you'll need to know:

  • Why sponsorship ROI is harder to prove than digital advertising ROI
  • The three-layer measurement framework: reach, engagement, conversion
  • Why sponsorship often exceeds ticketing as a revenue source

Test yourself: Can you calculate CPM (cost per thousand impressions) and explain why it matters?

Challenge 2: Analyze Sports Betting as a Growth Driver

The Problem: Sports betting operators like FanDuel and DraftKings are now among the top 10 sports sponsors globally. Why? And why do they pay 20-30% premiums vs. traditional categories?

What you'll need to know:

  • What "closed-loop attribution" means and why it matters
  • How betting increases fan engagement (40% more viewing time)
  • The regulatory landscape affecting sports betting partnerships

Test yourself: Can you explain why betting sponsors get better ROI data than traditional sponsors?

Challenge 3: Design Activation Plans That Deliver ROI

The Problem: A brand pays $10M for jersey sponsorship rights. But rights fees are just the beginning. How much should they spend on activation? What channels? How do they measure success?

What you'll need to know:

  • The 2:1 or 3:1 activation-to-rights fee ratio
  • What sponsorship "activation" actually means (beyond logo placement)
  • How to project ROI for CFO approval

Test yourself: Can you name 3 activation channels and explain how each drives measurable value?

Challenge 4: Calculate ROI with Imperfect Data

The Problem: You see LeBron's Nike jersey in June. You buy new shoes in September. Was that BECAUSE of the sponsorship? Or would you have bought them anyway? How do brands justify spend when causation is hard to prove?

What you'll need to know:

  • Why correlation doesn't equal causation in sponsorship
  • Frameworks for quantifying value with messy attribution
  • How to build ROI projections that CFOs actually approve

Test yourself: Can you calculate ROI if you know reach, engagement rate, conversion rate, and average purchase value?

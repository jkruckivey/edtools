### SCENE 4: TENSION (2:00-2:45)
**Visual:** Chart with Q4 highlighted in contrasting color

**Narration:**
"Act two: Tension. This is where something unexpected happens, where reality deviates from the setup. In our example, Q4 revenue drops 22% below Q3, putting the annual target at risk. Tension creates the reason your audience should care. Without tension, there's no story, just a status report."

**On-screen example:**
- Q4 bar changes to red
- Annotation: "-22% from Q3 (missed forecast by $2.1M)"
- Dotted line shows expected trajectory
- Gap between expected and actual highlighted

**Narration:**
"Effective tension isn't about drama. It's about showing the gap between expectation and reality. The contrast in color makes the drop immediately visible. The annotation quantifies the impact in dollars, not just percentages. The dotted forecast line shows what should have happened. Your viewer now understands the problem in five seconds."

Or it might argue for accelerating the new product launch to recover lost revenue."

## Element 8: Tiles â€” Three Story Types

**Uplimit Implementation:**
1. Select **Tiles** element
2. Configure as 1x3 horizontal layout
3. Copy content below:

**Tile 1:**

**Title:** Declarative Stories

**Description:**
State what happened with authority and clarity. Declarative stories work best for status updates, reporting dashboards, and situations where your audience needs shared understanding of current state. Example: "Q3 customer acquisition exceeded targets by 12%, driven by organic search traffic." Use this mode when the data speaks for itself and stakeholders trust your interpretation.

---

**Tile 2:**

**Title:** Exploratory Stories

**Description:**
Invite your audience to discover patterns with you. Exploratory stories work best for complex datasets with multiple interpretations, cross-functional meetings where perspectives differ, or situations requiring collaborative sensemaking. Example: "Q3 acquisition spiked, but retention rates haven't changed. Let's examine whether we're attracting the right customer segments." Use this mode when you want stakeholder engagement and co-creation.

---

**Tile 3:**

**Title:** Argumentative Stories

**Description:**
Build a case for a specific decision or action. Argumentative stories work best for budget requests, strategic recommendations, or situations where you need stakeholder commitment. Example: "Q3 acquisition growth masks a retention problem. We should shift 30% of acquisition budget to onboarding improvements." Use this mode when you have a clear recommendation and need buy-in.

## From Construction to Audience Adaptation

You've constructed setup-tension-resolution narratives for four datasets. Each time, you made choices about emphasis, framing, and resolution that transformed neutral charts into purposeful stories. A CFO scanning a monthly board deck needs the five-second version with the financial implication front and center. A product manager in a working session needs enough detail to identify root causes and explore solutions. A frontline team implementing changes needs concrete operational specifics translated from the strategic framing. Same data, same narrative arc, different execution.

The next tool demonstrates this adaptation challenge. You'll see a single visualization adapted for three stakeholder types: executive leadership, operational managers, and technical specialists.

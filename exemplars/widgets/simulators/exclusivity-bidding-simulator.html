Exclusivity vs Split Rights

When is more money NOT the better deal? A lesson in risk-adjusted value.

The Scenario (Illustrative)

You're commissioner of a major hockey league. Your country's broadcast rights are expiring. Three types of companies have submitted bids. Do you take the safer exclusive deal or chase the higher headline number with a split package?

Regional Broadcaster (Rogers-style): $450M/yr
Digital Sports Streamer (DAZN-style): $400M/yr
Tech Platform (Apple TV+-style): $700M exclusive / $300M streaming only
Option A: Exclusive Deal
$700M

Tech Platform gets all national rights — regional, streaming, playoffs.

Tech Platform (all rights) +$700M
Guaranteed Revenue $700M/yr
Why This Works
One partner, one app, one relationship. Fans know where to watch. Major tech platforms have deep pockets and are unlikely to exit mid-deal.
Option B: Split Package
$1.15B*

Regional Broadcaster + Digital Streamer + Tech Platform. More revenue on paper.

Regional Broadcaster +$450M
Digital Streamer +$400M
Tech Platform +$300M
Coordination costs -$40M
Fan fragmentation -$60M
Risk-Adjusted Revenue $1.05B/yr
Structural Risk
Multi-partner deals increase exposure to partner instability. If any partner exits mid-contract, you lose their contribution and scramble for replacement coverage. In a best-case scenario, all partners remain stable, and the league captures the full $1.05B value.
One Possible Outcome: Partner Exit

In one plausible scenario, a streaming partner pulls out mid-contract citing unsustainable losses. The $400M/year national broadcast deal evaporates. A replacement broadcaster picks up the games at a discount ($250M). New annual revenue: $960M — still more than the exclusive deal, but with significant operational complexity and uncertainty.

The Strategic Lesson

"More money" isn't always "better deal." Risk-adjusted value matters. In the downside scenario, the $1.15B headline became $960M — roughly 37% more than the guaranteed deal, but with significantly higher operational complexity and uncertainty.

Real-world parallel: The NFL splits its rights (CBS, FOX, NBC, ESPN, Amazon) because it has the market power to manage complexity. Smaller leagues often benefit from exclusive deals that guarantee stability.

Question to consider: How would you weigh the potential upside against the operational risk?

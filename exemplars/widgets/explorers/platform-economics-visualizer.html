Your Role

You're a media executive evaluating whether to bid on sports broadcasting rights. You'll model two scenarios: Linear TV (traditional cable with declining subscribers) vs. Streaming (digital platform starting from scratch). Adjust rights costs, subscriber bases, and growth rates to see how economics evolve over 10 years.

Before You Start: What This Model Simplifies
These assumptions make the model explorable but less realistic:
  • Smooth growth: Subscriber change is modeled as steady — no external shocks, content hits/flops, or competitive disruption
  • No competition: Model assumes you operate in isolation — no rival platforms affecting pricing or churn
  • Simplified costs: Real costs are hybrid (marketing, tech investment, rights renegotiation) — not purely fixed or variable
  • Single pricing tier: No ad-supported, bundles, or freemium options that real platforms use

The numbers are illustrative, not forecasts. Company names are used for context; scenarios are hypothetical and do not represent real financial data.

You can adjust all assumptions once inside the simulator.

Annual Decline (%) ? Cord-cutting rate — how fast people cancel cable each year. At 8%, you lose half your subscribers in ~8 years. This is linear TV's existential threat. 8%
Streaming Inputs
Rights Cost ($/yr) ? Annual content licensing fee. Streaming platforms often pay less than linear TV but must build audience from scratch. Apple pays ~$250M/yr for MLS. $50M
Monthly Price ($) ? What subscribers pay per month. Higher price = more revenue per user, but slower growth. Most sports streaming is $15-25/month. $20
Net Monthly Growth (%) ? New subscribers minus cancellations. 2% monthly = ~27% annual growth. Negative means you're shrinking. This is the key metric for streaming viability. 2%

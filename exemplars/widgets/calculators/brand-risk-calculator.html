Risk profiles are illustrative. Actual decisions depend on specific brand positioning, stakeholder relationships, and market context.

Premium Brands (High Equity)
More to lose from controversy
  • Large customer base sensitive to values
  • Employee pride tied to brand reputation
  • Media scrutiny amplifies any misstep
  • Decades of brand equity at risk
  • Result: Conservative approach justified
Challenger Brands (Low Equity)
Less to lose, more to gain
  • Smaller, less values-driven customer base
  • Employees expect aggressive growth tactics
  • Lower media attention = less amplification
  • Limited existing equity to protect
  • Result: Higher risk tolerance rational
The LIV Golf Paradox: Raw ROI was positive for many potential sponsors — Saudi funding made economics attractive. But for premium brands like Rolex, BMW, and major airlines, the risk-adjusted ROI was likely negative once boycott probability, employee sentiment, and brand equity damage were factored in. This explains why high-profile brands declined despite attractive financial terms.
Key Takeaway

Sponsorship ROI isn't just about the numbers on paper. Controversy costs — customer defection, employee discontent, and brand damage — can flip a profitable deal into a net loss. Premium brands rationally avoid controversial properties that challenger brands can embrace. The "right" decision depends on how much brand equity you have to protect.
